Story overview

A house that breathes. A mirror that warns. A river that isn’t just water.

Ava Parker just wanted to sneak to the Tennessee River. But the Parker farmhouse — alive, musical, and moody — felt it. And so did the mirror that holds their grandmother Autumn’s spirit.

Now the mirror is cracking, the walls are full of rushing water, and her older sister Aubrey is done cleaning up after Ava’s magic.

The sisters — responsible Aubrey, sensitive Abigail, unimpressed Alicia, and wild youngest Ava — have to figure out what the mirror is warning them about before the river outside the farm decides to come inside.

What’s waking up?

  • The Parker house acts on its own (doors, clocks, breakfast).
  • Grandmother Autumn appears in mirrors to warn them.
  • Ava’s trip to the river “stirred it.”
  • Water sounds inside the walls — like a river waiting.
  • Asher Blackwood might know the river’s secrets.

Chapter One – The Mirror

I dropped your actual pages in here so people can preview it right on the site. You can add more chapters later.

Welcome to my world—me and my sisters here in Marion County, Tennessee. The Parker farm sprawls across a hundred acres overlooking the Tennessee River, a place that feels like an escape from ordinary life. But nothing about this house is ordinary. From the road, the farmhouse looks long dead. Shutters hang crooked, paint peels in strips, and weeds claw up the porch like fingers desperate to drag the whole place into the earth... Ava had never feared the mirrors. Until last night. She stood in front of the long hallway glass, arms crossed, staring at a reflection that wasn’t quite hers... “You stirred it,” the ghost whispered. “And now it stirs for you.” A crack split across the mirror’s top corner, jagged as lightning. Somewhere deep in the walls, water dripped. One drop. Then another. Then the sound of rushing—like a river pressing against stone, waiting to break free.

Chapter Two – The River Knows

I couldn’t shake the image of Grandmother’s reflection from last night—the disappointment burning in her eyes like candle smoke. Being the youngest of five meant constant trouble. Always. And today—my first day of high school—I could already feel the universe plotting against me. The kitchen was alive. The bread browned itself. The kettle poured. My sisters filled every corner. “We need to talk about last night,” Aubrey said. Water rushed through the walls — then stopped. Later, at the bridge, the river boy was waiting. “Asher… how are you doing that?”
